What is an Interactive Oral Assessment?

Clarifying what IOAs are ‚Äî and what they're not.

An IOA is a structured, time-limited, professional conversation between a student and an assessor. The student demonstrates their understanding of unit content by discussing, explaining, and responding to questions ‚Äî much like a workplace consultation, case discussion, or professional review.

‚úì IOAs are

  • Professional conversations
  • Structured around authentic scenarios
  • Testing depth of understanding
  • Assessing ability to think on feet
  • An opportunity to probe and follow up

‚úó IOAs are not

  • Traditional oral exams or vivas
  • Presentations or speeches
  • Communication skills tests
  • Memorisation exercises
  • Informal chats without structure

The critical difference between an IOA and a traditional oral exam is authenticity. In an IOA, the conversation mirrors a real professional interaction. A nursing student might discuss a patient handover. An engineering student might defend a design decision to a simulated client. An accounting student might explain a tax strategy to a colleague.

This authenticity is what makes IOAs both more engaging for students and more valid as assessment ‚Äî you're testing whether they can use their knowledge, not just recall it.

Is IOA right for my unit?

Application of knowledge: IOAs assess higher-order thinking (Bloom's analyse, evaluate, create) because students must explain reasoning in real time ‚Äî they can't just reproduce memorised content.

Academic integrity: IOAs are inherently integrity-proof. The student must demonstrate understanding live ‚Äî AI tools, contract cheating, and plagiarism are not viable strategies.

Marking time: Research shows up to 60% reduction in marking time (Shaeri et al.) because assessment and feedback happen simultaneously.

Scale: Griffith University has successfully run IOAs with 800+ students using parallel scheduling, trained sessional markers, and structured rubrics. Cohort size is not a barrier.

Equity: Research by Davey et al. found no significant equity gaps across demographics in IOA performance.

Building Your Rubric

Define criteria that match your IOA's authentic context.

Key principle: IOA rubrics should assess the quality of thinking, not the quality of speaking. Unless communication is explicitly a learning outcome, avoid penalising presentation style, fluency, or accent.

Common IOA rubric dimensions:

‚Ä¢ Content accuracy ‚Äî correctness of knowledge demonstrated

‚Ä¢ Depth of reasoning ‚Äî ability to explain "why" not just "what"

‚Ä¢ Application ‚Äî connecting theory to the specific scenario

‚Ä¢ Adaptability ‚Äî response when the assessor probes, challenges, or pivots

‚Ä¢ Professional communication ‚Äî only if this is an explicit learning outcome

Tip: Use 3‚Äì4 criteria maximum. Each should be distinguishable at the pass/fail boundary ‚Äî if you can't describe what a "fail" vs "pass" looks like for a criterion, it's too vague.

Scheduling formula: For a 10-minute IOA with 2-minute transitions, you can assess 5 students per hour. A cohort of 100 = ~20 hours of assessment time, which can be parallelised across multiple assessors.

Online delivery: Microsoft Teams is the standard at Deakin. Use the booking system or SOTS for scheduling. Students join their allocated timeslot. Record the session for moderation purposes.

Face-to-face: Book rooms with a waiting area. Consider staggered arrival times to avoid queue anxiety. Have a backup plan for no-shows (offer a reschedule window).

Recording: Check your faculty's requirements. At minimum, record a moderation sample (e.g., every 5th student, or all borderline cases). Store recordings per your data management plan.

Multiple assessors: Run a calibration session before marking begins. Have all assessors mark the same 2‚Äì3 students independently, then compare and discuss. This is essential for consistency.

Preparing Students

Students perform better when they understand what to expect.

Research consistently shows that student anxiety about oral assessment decreases significantly when they receive clear preparation and practice opportunities.

Students need to know:

Accessibility adjustments: IOAs can be more accessible than written exams for some students (e.g., those with dyslexia). However, they may present challenges for students with speech-related disabilities, severe anxiety disorders, or certain neurodiverse conditions.

Adjustments to consider:

‚Ä¢ Extended time (e.g., 15 min instead of 10)

‚Ä¢ Provision of questions 5 minutes before the IOA

‚Ä¢ Option to have a support person present

‚Ä¢ Written alternative for students with documented speech/communication disabilities

‚Ä¢ Online option even when others are face-to-face

Equity note: Research by Davey et al. found no significant equity gaps in IOA performance across gender, language background, or socioeconomic status.
