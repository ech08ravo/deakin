What is an Interactive Oral Assessment?

Definitions, distinctions, and the evidence base.

An IOA is a structured, time-limited, professional conversation between a student and an assessor. The student demonstrates their understanding of unit content by discussing, explaining, and responding to questions — much like a workplace consultation, case discussion, or professional review.

IOAs are

  • Professional conversations
  • Structured around authentic scenarios
  • Testing depth of understanding
  • Assessing ability to think on feet
  • An opportunity to probe and follow up

IOAs are not

  • Traditional oral exams or vivas
  • Presentations or speeches
  • Communication skills tests
  • Memorisation exercises
  • Informal unstructured chats

Key distinction: Authenticity

The critical difference between an IOA and a traditional oral exam is authenticity. In an IOA, the conversation mirrors a real professional interaction. A nursing student discusses a patient handover. An engineering student defends a design decision to a simulated client. An accounting student explains a tax strategy to a colleague.

This authenticity is what makes IOAs both more engaging for students and more valid as assessment — you're testing whether they can use their knowledge, not just recall it.

Is IOA Right for My Unit?

Your answers are saved to your export.

Check any that apply:

Application of knowledge: IOAs assess higher-order thinking (Bloom's analyse, evaluate, create) because students must explain reasoning in real time.

Academic integrity: IOAs are inherently integrity-proof. The student must demonstrate understanding live — AI tools, contract cheating, and plagiarism are not viable strategies (Ward 2024).

Marking time: Up to 60% reduction because assessment and feedback happen simultaneously (Shaeri et al.).

Scale: Griffith University successfully ran IOAs with 800+ students using parallel scheduling, trained sessional markers, and structured rubrics.

Equity: No significant gaps across demographics (Davey et al. 2025).

Duration equivalence: 5 min = targeted topic. 10 min = ~3-4,000 words. 15 min = complex scenarios. For a 1,500-word essay replacement, 10-12 minutes is appropriate (Dean 2025).

Step 4: Classification

Assessment Task: Standard extensions (up to 7 days, 14 with Access Plan). Most flexible. Student books their slot.

EOUA (End of Unit Assessment): Oral/performative category. Requires Faculty Assessment Committee approval. No extensions — special consideration applies instead. Per Deakin Assessment Clause 65.

Examination: Scheduled during exam period. Most restrictive. Unusual for IOAs unless replacing a traditional exam.

Recommendation: If flexibility matters, use Assessment Task. If you need the scheduling structure of an exam period, use EOUA with Faculty Committee approval.

Building Your Rubric

Assess thinking, not speaking. 3-4 criteria max.

Key principle: Assess the quality of thinking, not the quality of speaking. Unless communication is explicitly a learning outcome, avoid penalising presentation style, fluency, or accent.

Common rubric dimensions

CriterionWhat it assessesUse when
Content accuracyCorrectness of knowledge demonstratedAlways
Depth of reasoningAbility to explain "why" not just "what"Always
Application to scenarioConnecting theory to the specific contextScenario-based IOAs
AdaptabilityResponse when assessor probes, challenges, or pivotsWhen using challenge prompts
Professional communicationAppropriate tone, audience awarenessOnly if explicit LO

1. What does excellent look like? Describe the HD student's performance.

2. What does just passing look like? Describe the minimum acceptable performance.

3. What does failing look like? Describe clearly unacceptable performance.

4. What lies in between? Fill in Distinction and Credit levels.

5. Link to observable behaviours: Use verbs — "identifies", "explains", "adapts", "integrates" — not vague terms like "demonstrates understanding".

6. Build in your feedback: Write descriptors as feedback-ready statements. Instead of "good analysis", write "accurately identifies the key legal implications and connects them to the client's specific circumstances".

Delivery and recording

Before marking begins: Run a calibration session. All assessors mark the same 2-3 recorded IOAs independently, then compare and discuss. Document agreed interpretations for borderline cases. This is essential for consistency.

During marking: Record a moderation sample — at minimum every 5th student, or all borderline cases. 20% sample is standard.

After marking: Review sample across assessors. Check consistency with rubric interpretation. Report to Assessment Panel per faculty process. Debrief with teaching team: what worked, what to adjust.
