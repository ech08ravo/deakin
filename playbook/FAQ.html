Getting Started

Start with the evidence: universities report higher student satisfaction, better learning outcomes, enhanced academic integrity, and more engaging assessment for staff. Consider beginning with a small pilot cohort to demonstrate feasibility before full implementation.

Present IOAs as a solution to current challenges: academic integrity concerns with AI, development of employability skills, and inclusive assessment practices. Frame it as authentic assessment that better prepares students for professional contexts.

Time & Workload

Initial setup requires 5-6 weeks for scenario development, rubric creation, and staff training. However, ongoing delivery time matches or improves upon traditional marking loads. Universities report that while the upfront investment is significant, the long-term efficiency and teaching insights make it worthwhile.

Yes, once implemented. A 15-minute IOA provides equivalent assessment depth to a 3,000-word essay but eliminates extensive written feedback requirements. Marking happens in real-time during the conversation, and feedback is delivered immediately rather than requiring separate written responses.

Use scenario-based assessment rather than specific questions, ensuring each conversation is unique while maintaining consistent evaluation criteria. The interactive nature makes it virtually impossible for students to share specific answers because each conversation develops differently.

Implement pre-assessment calibration meetings, shared rubric interpretation protocols, and real-time consultation processes during assessment periods. Record IOAs for moderation purposes and conduct regular cross-assessor reviews. The recording provides objective evidence for consistency monitoring.

While intensive during assessment periods, IOAs eliminate extensive written feedback requirements and provide immediate completion of marking tasks. Many staff find the real-time nature of IOAs more satisfying than traditional marking, and the immediate feedback to students improves teaching relationships.

Implementation Checklist:
  • Week 0 (Before trimester starts): Scenario development, rubric creation, staff training, technical setup
  • Weeks 2-6: Student briefing materials, practice opportunities, booking system setup
  • Weeks 6-10: Student preparation begins, exemplar recordings available
  • Weeks 10-11: Final staff calibration, technical testing, student Q&A sessions
  • Weeks 11-12 (Study Week): Final preparations, emergency protocols confirmed

Scenario Design

Design authentic workplace situations where students act as professionals (consultants, advisers, specialists) responding to realistic challenges. For STEM: environmental impact assessments, engineering design justifications, genetics counselling sessions, or technical consulting scenarios. The scenario should require content knowledge application, not just recall.

Focus on conversation facilitation, active listening, and curiosity-driven questioning rather than repeating identical questions for every student. Develop skills in real-time rubric application and supportive assessment practices that help students demonstrate their best understanding.

Assessment & Rubrics

Effective rubrics function as conversation guides that align with course learning outcomes. They should prompt assessors towards higher-order questions when students demonstrate understanding, and focus on content knowledge and application rather than communication skills (unless specifically required). Include clear progression from basic understanding to sophisticated analysis.

Use detailed rubrics focused on learning outcomes, implement blind moderation of recorded assessments, and establish clear protocols for difficult conversations. The recording provides accountability and evidence for addressing any consistency issues.

Maximum 20 IOAs per day with mandatory breaks after every 3-4 sessions. Mental fatigue becomes significant beyond this limit. Many universities find 12-15 per day more sustainable for maintaining assessment quality and staff wellbeing.

All teaching team members should experience IOAs from both student and assessor perspectives, practise with actual rubrics, and participate in calibration sessions. Training should cover conversation facilitation skills, rubric interpretation, and moderation protocols. Plan for 6-8 hours initial training plus usual moderation meetings.

Provide additional practice opportunities and pair less confident assessors with experienced colleagues initially. Most staff find IOAs more engaging than traditional marking once they develop conversation facilitation skills.

Establish clear backup protocols, rescheduling procedures for genuine technical issues, and emergency contact procedures.

Provide extensive practice opportunities, recorded exemplars, and clear rubric explanations. Emphasise that IOAs assess content knowledge, not communication skills. Research shows student anxiety decreases significantly once they experience the conversational format. Consider low-stakes practice sessions early in the course.

IOAs work best as part of a diverse assessment portfolio. They're particularly effective for demonstrating applied knowledge and professional competencies. Consider which learning outcomes are best assessed through conversation versus other methods.

Student Support

Provide comprehensive briefing materials including scenario descriptions, sample rubrics, recorded exemplars, and practice opportunities. Students need a minimum of 3-4 weeks advance notice with clear expectations. The research shows students perform significantly better when given detailed preparation resources.

Consider what's authentic for the professional scenario. Many successful IOAs allow supporting materials (notes, data, presentations) that mirror real workplace practice. This shifts focus from memorisation to knowledge application and synthesis.

IOAs can be more inclusive than written assessments because they allow real-time clarification and multiple ways to demonstrate understanding. Work with disability services for appropriate accommodations. Research shows IOAs often benefit international students because they can clarify misunderstandings immediately.

Use supportive questioning techniques, provide thinking time when needed, and remember that content knowledge is being assessed, not presentation skills. Some students need encouragement to engage in dialogue rather than delivering prepared monologues.

Use follow-up questions to explore their reasoning and provide opportunities for self-correction. The conversational format allows students to clarify or revise their thinking, which actually provides better assessment of understanding than written responses where mistakes cannot be corrected.

Universities successfully implement IOAs with 800+ students using distributed scheduling, multiple assessors, and standardised rubrics. Griffith University manages 750+ students per unit with sustained positive outcomes. The key is systematic organisation and adequate staff resources.

Staff consistently report that IOAs provide clearer insights into student learning and thinking processes. Many describe IOAs as more engaging and informative than traditional marking, leading to improved teaching practices and better understanding of student needs.
